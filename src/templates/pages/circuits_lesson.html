Recap: Common Quantum Gates

You can review the most common quantum gates below:

Identity Gate

No change to qubit state

Pauli-X Gate

Rotation around x-axis by 180°

Pauli-Y Gate

Rotation around y-axis by 180°

Pauli-Z Gate

Rotation around z-axis by 180°

S Gate

Rotation around z-axis by 90°

T Gate

Rotation around z-axis by 45°

Hadamard Gate

Rotation around the x-axis by 90°, followed by a rotation around the z-axis by 180°

Control Node

Applies a gate to a target qubit if the control qubit is in state |1>

Anti-control Node

Applies a gate to a target qubit if the control qubit is in state |0>

SWAP Gate

Swaps the states of two qubits

Series

Quantum circuits 'flow' from left to right - gates are applied in this order. The diagram below is a basic, example that we will look at now.

In a circuit, each qubit is represented by a horizontal circuit line. Our diagram contains a single qubit that is labelled \(q[0]\). In a circuit, all qubits begin initially in the computational basis state of \(|0\rangle\). This circuit is read (and executed) from left to right. The two grey areas on the circuit represent 'moments', moment 1 (m1) and moment 2 (m2) respectively.

Lets go through this circuit step by step. We start with \(q[0]\) being in the \(|0\rangle\) state. The first moment (m1, containing a Pauli-X gate) then gets applied to \(q[0]\) which results in the \(|1\rangle\) state. The second moment (m2, containing another Pauli-X gate) then gets applied to \(q[0]\) which results in the \(|0\rangle\) state.

Parallel

We will now look at circuits with multiple qubits, where operations can be applied in 'parallel'. In these circuits, a moment contains all operations to be appliced/executed at a single point in time, at the same time. We draw parallel circuit lines for each qubit in a multi-qubit circuit, and qubits are labelled \(q[0]\) to \(q[n]\), for \(n\) amount of qubits in a circuit. See the diagram below:

In this diagram, we have moment 1 (m1) and moment 2 (m2). Moment 1 contains two Pauli-X gates, one on \(q[0]\), and one on \(q[1]\). When moment 1 is executed, these two gates get applied at the same time to each qubit respectively. Additionally, this moment contains no operations on \(q[2]\), so nothing happens to this qubit in moment 1. When moment 2 is executed, the Pauli-X gate on \(q[0]\) and the Pauli-X gate on \([q2]\) get executed at the same time. There are no operations on \(q[1]\) in moment 2, so nothing happens to this qubit.

The entire system (of the 3 qubits) can be represented as \(|q[1]q[2]q[3]\rangle\):

Before moment 1: \(|000\rangle\)

After moment 1: \(|110\rangle\)

After moment 2: \(|011\rangle\)

Another Example

Again, in the diagram below we have two moments, moment 1 (m1) and moment 2 (m2). This time, however, we have a controlled SWAP gate in moment 2.

Moment 1 is similar to what we have seen before, and after moment 1 the system is in the \(|110\rangle\) state. Looking at moment 2 now, we have a control node on \(q[0]\) connected to a SWAP gate over \(q[1]\) and \(q[2]\). As the system is in the \(|110\rangle\) state before moment 2, when moment 2 is executed, the control node is triggered. This means the SWAP gate is executed, and the states of \(q[1]\) and \(q[2]\) are swapped. Nothing happens to \(q[0]\) as a control node does not perform any transformations on the control qubit. After moment 2, the system is in the \(|101\rangle\) state.
