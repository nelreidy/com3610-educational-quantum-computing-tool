Introduction

This lesson is designed to introduce you to the world of quantum computing, and how it differs from classical computing. We will introduce the qubit, and how it is able to perform computations that are impossible for classical bits. You will also learn about the unique properties of qubits, such as superposition and entanglement. By the end of this lesson, you will have a solid understanding of the basics of quantum computing.

How much do I need to know to start?

Do not worry! This lesson assumes no background knowledge in quantum computing. We will start from the basics and build up from there. If you have a basic understanding of classical computing, that will be helpful, but it is not required. We will cover all the necessary concepts in this lesson.

Introduction: Classical Computing

Before we dive into the world of quantum computing, let's take a moment to review classical computing. Classical computing is the type of computing that most of us are familiar with - we rely on it, and use it day in, day out. The fact that you can view this lesson is made possible through classical computing.

Information in Classical Computing

The computers we are farmiliar with fundamentally come down to represeting, sending and storing information. In classical computing, information is represented as bits. A bit is the smallest unit of information, and physically, you can think of it as an electrical signal of either LOW or HIGH voltage - 0 or 1.

Combinations of bits can be used to represent more complex information. For example, a byte is made up of 8 bits, and can represent a number between 0 and 255. This is how classical computers store and process information - by manipulating bits.

The power of classical computing comes from the ability to manipulate bits in a logical way. By performing operations on bits, we can perform complex calculations, store data, and run programs. This is a very high-level overview on classical computing, and there is far more detail and theory behind classical computing than this lesson can go into.

What is a Qubit?

In classical computing we talked about electrical voltages physically representing bits. In quantum computing, we use different physical systems to represent information, that behave differently to deterministic (classical) systems.

Definition

Deterministic: An outcome is completley predictable given the initial state and inputs. For example, if you give a calculator an input of '2+2', you can predict the exact output (4) without having to see the calculator's output.

A simple example of a different system used for quantum computing is electrons with spin UP and spin DOWN states (\(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\)). A single electron in this system represents a single unit of information - a qubit. The primary and fundamental difference between this, and the classical system of electrical signals, is that quantum systems are non-deterministic. This will be explained further soon.

Definition

Non-deterministic: An outcome can differ, even when given the same initial state and inputs. Non-deterministic systems have random or probablistic properties that give them this behaviour.

What is a Qubit: Review

So far, we have learnt that in quantum computing we have different physical systems than in classical computing. This also means quantum computing uses different units of information than classical - the qubit.

A qubit can be visualised as a point on the surface of a sphere, where the north pole represents \(|0\rangle\) and the south pole represents \(|1\rangle\). This sphere is called a Bloch Sphere (see the diagram), and is a standard visualisation used in quantum computing.

Finally, we know that classical systems are deterministic, while quantum systems are non-deterministic. The next sections will explain exactly what this means, and why this is such an important quality of quantum computing.

Superposition

Superposition is one of the unique properties of quantum systems that makes quantum computing so powerful. Similar to classical computing, a qubit can be in one of two distinct states. However, unlike classical computing a qubit may also be in a superposition of both these two states at once.

Think of the physical systems used for each type of computing - in classical computing, of course you can only have 0 or 1. You can have ON or OFF, but nothing inbetween. While in quantum computing, think of the Bloch sphere, you can have a point at either the north pole (\(|0\rangle\)), or the south pole (\(|1\rangle\)), but you can also have a point anywhere else on the surface of the sphere, where this would be a superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩.

Definition

Superposition: The unique property of a qubit to exist in a state that is a combination of both \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\).

An aside on notation

To formalise everything we have discussed up to this point, and to continue talking about superposition, we need some notation.

In quantum computing, we use a notation called 'Dirac notation' to represent qubit states. This notation is used to represent the state of a qubit, and is written as \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\). You may have noticed us using this notation already. The \(|v\rangle\) combination of symbols is called a 'ket', and it represents a vector. The Bloch sphere helps us visualise and represent vectors, which correlate to qubit states. See the diagram - \(|0\rangle\) represents \((0,0,1)\) and \(|1\rangle\) represents \((0,0,-1)\)

We can write out a quantum state formally as:

$$|\phi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$$

Where \(\phi\) is the vector state, and \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are complex numbers representing the probability of measuring the qubit in the \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) states, respectively.

Finally, \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are referred to as the computational basis states.

Back to superposition

Let's consider the following example:

$$|\phi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|0\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|1\rangle$$

This state represents a qubit in a superposition of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) with equal probability. Where the coefficients \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) are complex numbers that represent the probability of measuring the qubit in the \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) states, respectively.

Intuitively, on a Bloch sphere, this point lies on the equator exactly between the north and south poles, as shown in the diagram. This specific state appears frequently and is called the plus state: \(|+\rangle\).

Additional common states

Apart from the two computational basis states \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\), and the state we saw previously, \(|+\rangle\), there are also 3 more common states that have their own symbols - the \(|-\rangle\), \(|i\rangle\) and \(|-i\rangle\) states.

Measurement

A really important distinction should be made at this point. Although a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states, it can only be measured as either \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\). Measurement forces the qubit to collapse into one of the two computational basis states.

The Schrödinger's Cat thought experiment can help conceptualise this. In the experiment, a cat, a vial of poison, a hammer, a radioactive source and a Geiger counter are placed in a sealed box. If the Geiger counter detects a single atom from the radioactive source decaying, the hammer is triggered to shatter the vial of poison, killing the cat.

The idea is that the event of the radioactive source decaying is random and may or may not occour.

The cat is in a superposition of both alive and dead states until the box is opened and the cat is observed. Once the box is opened, the cat must be alive or dead, but not in a superposition of both.

Entanglement

Entanglement is another unique quantum property that involves multiple qubits. So far we have spoken about systems only involving a single qubit, where we have two computational basis states - \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\).

If we had a quantum system with two qubits, there would be four computational basis states - \(|00\rangle\), \(|01\rangle\), \(|10\rangle\) and \(|11\rangle\). A pair of qubits can be said to be in a superposition of these four states. For example:

$$ |\phi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|00\rangle + \frac{1}{2}|01\rangle + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}|10\rangle + \frac{1}{4}|11\rangle $$

The probability of measuring the two qubits is \(\frac{1}{2}\) for \(|00\rangle\), \(\frac{1}{4}\) for \(|01\rangle\), \(\frac{3}{16}\) for \(|10\rangle\) and \(\frac{1}{16}\) for \(|11\rangle\).

To generalise this, for \(n\)-qubits of a system, there are \(2^{n}\) computational basis states, where the system is in a superposition of the \(2^{n}\) computational basis states.

Entanglement

Consider this example:

$$|\phi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|00\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|11\rangle$$

Notice how there is no possibility of measuring either \(|01\rangle\) or \(|10\rangle\), and additionally if you measure the first qubit as 0 or 1, without measuring the second, you can deduce its state. If we measured \(|0?\rangle\), the only possibility is that the second qubit is \(|0\rangle\).

This is the concept of entanglement - where the state of one qubit is correlated to the state of another qubit.
