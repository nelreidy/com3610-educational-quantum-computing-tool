Introduction

Quantum computing operates at the intersection of physics and computer science, leveraging quantum mechanics to perform computations in ways that classical computers cannot. However, due to the current limitations of quantum hardware, many quantum algorithms are often tested and developed using simulations before being executed on real quantum devices.

Quantum Hardware vs. Simulation

Quantum computations can be executed in two primary environments: on actual quantum processors or using classical simulations that mimic quantum behavior.

  • Quantum Hardware – Physical quantum computers, such as those built by IBM, Google, and Rigetti, use superconducting qubits, trapped ions, or other quantum systems to perform calculations. These devices provide real quantum behavior but are limited by noise, decoherence, and hardware constraints.
  • Quantum Simulation – Classical computers can simulate quantum mechanics up to a certain number of qubits. This is useful for algorithm development, debugging, and educational purposes but becomes impractical for large-scale quantum computations due to exponential resource requirements.

Many quantum programming frameworks, such as Qiskit, Cirq, and Microsoft Quantum Development Kit, allow users to write quantum programs that can run both on simulators and real quantum hardware.

What Should I Know Before Starting?

To better understand the differences between quantum hardware and simulation, it helps to have a basic grasp of the following concepts:

  • Superposition – The ability of qubits to exist in multiple states simultaneously, unlike classical bits.
  • Entanglement – A phenomenon where two or more qubits become interconnected, meaning the state of one qubit instantly influences the other, no matter the distance between them.
  • Quantum Measurement – The act of measuring a qubit collapses its superposition into a definite state, affecting the outcome of computations.
  • Quantum Circuits – The sequence of quantum gates that manipulate qubits to perform computations.

If these concepts are new to you, consider starting with our Introduction to Quantum Computing lesson before proceeding.

Quantum Hardware

Quantum computers operate fundamentally differently from classical computers, leveraging quantum mechanics to process information. Unlike traditional systems, they do not use operating systems and instead rely on direct manipulation of qubits to execute quantum circuits.

What is a Qubit? (on an almost physical level)

Unlike classical computers, which use bits represented by high and low voltages (1s and 0s), quantum computers use qubits. Instead of just being 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a combination of both states at the same time due to superposition.

In classical computers, transistors use electrical voltage to store and manipulate bits. However, in quantum systems, qubits are typically implemented using one of: superconducting circuits, trapped ions, or photonic systems.

Superconducting Circuit Systems

1

A superconducting circuit is just like a normal electrical circuit but made of superconducting material. This means it conducts electricity without resistance, so current can flow forever without losing energy.

2

Inside this circuit, we add something called a Josephson Junction—a tiny insulating barrier. This junction forces the current to flow in a quantum way, meaning it can flow in one direction or the other, or even both directions at the same time (superposition).

3

The direction of the current flow defines the qubit state:

  • Current flowing one way = |0⟩ (low energy state).
  • Current flowing the other way = |1⟩ (high energy state).
  • Both at once (superposition) = The qubit is in a mix of |0⟩ and |1⟩.
4

To control the qubit, we use microwave pulses. These pulses give the qubit energy to push the current in the direction we want. This is how we perform quantum operations like flipping a qubit from |0⟩ → |1⟩ or creating superposition.

5

In multi-qubit systems, each qubit consists of its own superconducting loop with a Josephson junction, forming distinct quantum states. Qubits interact through electric fields (capacitive coupling) or magnetic fields (inductive coupling), enabling entanglement and controlled quantum operations such as multi-qubit gates.

This technology is used by IBM, Google, and Rigetti because it allows for fast quantum operations, but the need for extreme cooling and suscpetibility to noise makes it challenging to scale.

Trapped Ion Quantum Systems

1

A trapped ion system consists of individual charged atoms (ions) that are suspended using electromagnetic fields inside a vacuum chamber. These ions are highly stable, preserving their quantum states for extended periods.

2

Each ion has multiple electronic energy levels, but we use two specific levels to represent a qubit:

3

The qubit states in a trapped ion system are defined as:

  • Lower energy state = |0⟩
  • Higher energy state = |1⟩
  • Superposition = The ion exists in a mix of |0⟩ and |1⟩.
4

To control the qubit, laser pulses are used to manipulate the ion’s quantum state. These pulses provide energy to transition between |0⟩ and |1⟩, allowing quantum operations like superposition and entanglement.

5

In multi-qubit systems, each qubit is an ion, and qubits interact through Coulomb forces (electrostatic repulsion). This enables entanglement and controlled multi-qubit gates, such as the CNOT Gate.

Photonic Quantum Systems

1

A photonic quantum system uses individual photons (particles of light) to represent qubits. Unlike other quantum systems that use matter-based qubits, photons travel at the speed of light and are naturally resistant to environmental noise.

2

Qubits in photonic systems are encoded using light properties, such as:

3

The quantum state of a photonic qubit is defined by:

  • Polarization – The direction in which the light wave oscillates (e.g., horizontal = |0⟩, vertical = |1⟩).
  • Phase – The wave’s position in its oscillation cycle, which can encode superposition states.
  • Path Encoding – Using beam splitters to direct photons into separate paths, representing different quantum states.
4

To manipulate photonic qubits, optical components such as beam splitters, wave plates, and phase shifters are used. These components change the quantum state of the photons, allowing quantum operations to be performed.

5

In multi-qubit systems, each qubit is an ion, and qubits interact through Coulomb forces (electrostatic repulsion). This enables entanglement and controlled multi-qubit gates, such as the CNOT Gate.

Running Programs on Quantum Hardware

Now that we understand the basics of how different quantum systems work, let's explore this basic overview of how run quantum programs run on real quantum hardware.

Recall that in classical computing, programs are executed on a Von Neumann architecture, which consists of a CPU, memory (RAM), registers, and storage. Instructions are fetched from memory, processed by the CPU, and data is temporarily stored in registers before being written back to memory or disk.

1

Quantum computers do not use a traditional operating system. Instead of a CPU and RAM, they rely on quantum processors (QPUs) that operate based on quantum mechanics. There is no central OS managing tasks dynamically; rather, quantum programs are sent as precompiled instructions directly to the quantum processor.

2

The architecture of a quantum system consists of:

  • Quantum Processing Unit (QPU) – The core quantum processor that executes quantum operations.
  • Classical Control System – Manages error correction, qubit initialization, and state readout.
  • Quantum Memory – Unlike classical RAM, quantum memory is ephemeral, meaning qubit states last only for a short time.
  • Cryogenic Systems – Most quantum processors (like superconducting qubits) require near absolute zero temperatures to function properly.
3

Quantum programs are written in quantum programming languages such as Qiskit, Cirq, or Quipper. The program is converted into a sequence of quantum gates, which are executed on the quantum processor. A classical computer handles the preprocessing and final result analysis.

4

Unlike classical computers, which store data in registers and RAM, quantum computers use qubits that hold information in superposition. Since quantum states are fragile, results must be measured before the qubits decohere. This means memory is not persistent, and intermediate results cannot be stored like in classical systems.

5

After execution, the quantum system collapses the qubits into a classical state through measurement. These results are then processed by a classical computer to interpret and analyze the computation.

The Current State of Quantum Hardware

Quantum hardware is still in the early stages of development, with companies racing to increase qubit counts and improve error correction. Most quantum computers are experimental and require cryogenic cooling to function.

Industry Leaders
  • IBM – One of the biggest players, offering 127-qubit and 433-qubit superconducting quantum processors.
  • Google – Achieved quantum supremacy in 2019, working on error-corrected quantum systems.
  • Rigetti Computing – Developing cloud-accessible superconducting quantum processors.
  • IonQ – Leading in trapped ion quantum computing with some of the highest qubit fidelity.
  • D-Wave – Specializes in quantum annealing for optimization problems.
Accessibility

The average person can access real quantum computers through cloud-based platforms:

  • IBM Quantum Experience – Free access to small quantum processors.
  • Amazon Braket – Cloud service offering access to multiple quantum hardware providers.
  • Microsoft Azure Quantum – Provides quantum hardware access through cloud computing.

While current quantum computers are still prone to errors and decoherence, rapid advancements are being made in scalability and fault tolerance. Access to larger systems is typically limited to research institutions and enterprise clients.

Quantum Simulation

Now that we know about the state of hardware-based quantum development, let's learn about how quantum simulation works and what tools we can use to simulate quantum circuits, algorithms, and programs.

What is Quantum Simulation?

Quantum simulation refers to the process of using classical computers to mimic the behavior of quantum systems. Since quantum hardware is still developing, simulations allow researchers to test quantum algorithms, study quantum mechanics, and develop applications without needing real quantum processors. Simulations run on classical CPUs and GPUs. As such, classical computers cannot store quantum states directly, and instead use:

  • State vector simulation – Stores the full quantum state of a system as a vector and applies operations directly.
  • Tensor network simulation – Uses a compressed representation to handle larger quantum systems more efficiently.
  • Density matrix simulation – Models noise and decoherence, making it useful for testing real-world conditions.

Simulation Limitations

Despite their usefulness, quantum simulations have limitations. The main challenges of quantum simulation include:

  • Exponential memory requirements – A system with n qubits requires storing 2ⁿ complex numbers, making large simulations impractical.
  • Slow performance for large qubit counts – Simulating more than 30–40 qubits becomes computationally expensive, even on supercomputers.
  • Limited ability to model quantum speedups – Classical simulations cannot fully replicate the quantum parallelism that makes quantum computing powerful.

Some Available Simulation Frameworks and Tools

Quantum simulations on classical hardware can be achieved using different approaches. There are generally two main types of quantum simulators: interactive UI-based circuit simulators, which provide a drag-and-drop interface, and programming frameworks, which allow developers to code quantum circuits, algorithms, and programs.

UI-Based Circuit Simulators

These simulators allow users to visually construct quantum circuits using a drag-and-drop interface, making them great for beginners and educational purposes. Examples include:

  • IBM Quantum Composer – A graphical quantum circuit builder available through IBM Quantum Experience.
  • Quirk – An interactive quantum circuit simulator for experimenting with quantum gates.
  • Quantum Playground – A web-based simulator for visualizing quantum states and circuits.
  • This web-apps very own Quantum Circuit simulator A simplified beginner friendly simulator with attached examples, tutorials, and a qiskit implementation.

Programming-Based Quantum Frameworks

These frameworks allow quantum circuits to be programmed using code, providing more flexibility and control over quantum simulations.

  • Qiskit – IBM’s high-performance simulator, supporting state-vector, noise modeling, and density matrix methods.
  • Cirq – Google’s quantum simulation library, used for developing quantum algorithms.
  • QuTiP – Focuses on open quantum systems and quantum mechanics research.
  • PennyLane – Used for quantum machine learning simulations.
  • Microsoft Quantum Development Kit (Q#) – Provides simulators for quantum algorithm testing.

IBM is one of the leading entities in quantum computing, particularly known for its advancements in superconducting qubits and quantum cloud services. Beyond hardware, IBM provides free and comprehensive educational resources, making quantum computing more accessible to learners worldwide. Introduction to Qiskit

Now that we understand quantum systems and simulation, it's time to start working with real quantum programming! To do this, we will be using Qiskit—one of the most widely used quantum programming frameworks.

What is Qiskit?

Qiskit (Quantum Information Science Kit) is an open-source quantum computing framework (Python library) developed by IBM. It allows us to:

  • Design and simulate quantum circuits on a classical computer.
  • Run quantum algorithms on real IBM quantum hardware via the cloud.
  • Use built-in quantum algorithms, visualization tools, and error mitigation techniques.

Why Qiskit?

We are using Qiskit because:

  • It is free and open-source—anyone can use it.
  • It is backed by IBM, one of the leaders in quantum computing.
  • It provides both simulation and real quantum hardware execution.
  • It has an active community and extensive documentation, making it beginner-friendly.

What Will We Learn?

In this tutorial, we will start by learning the basics of Qiskit, including:

  • Installing Qiskit and setting up the environment.
  • Creating and visualizing quantum circuits.
  • Simulating quantum algorithms on a local simulator.
  • Running quantum programs on real IBM quantum hardware.

⚠️ Qiskit Version Warning

Qiskit is known to have breaking changes between versions, which can lead to code incompatibility. This tutorial is designed for Qiskit version 1.4.0. If you are using a different version, some code may not work as expected.

Setting Up Your Environment for Qiskit

Before we start coding quantum circuits, we need to set up our environment and install Qiskit version 1.4.0. Follow these steps to ensure everything works correctly.

1

Ensure you have Python 3.8 or later installed. You can check your Python version by running: 

python --version

If you need to install Python, download it from the official site: python.org/downloads.

2

It's recommended to install Qiskit inside a virtual environment to avoid conflicts with other Python packages. 

python -m venv qiskit-env
source qiskit-env/bin/activate  # On Mac/Linux
qiskit-env\Scripts\activate     # On Windows

Once activated, your terminal should show (qiskit-env) before the command prompt.

3

Now, install Qiskit version 1.4.0 using pip: 

pip install qiskit==1.4.0

⚠️ Troubleshooting Installation Issues

If you encounter errors during installation, try the following:

  • Ensure you're using Python 3.8 or later.
  • Upgrade pip: pip install --upgrade pip
  • Use a clean virtual environment before installing Qiskit.
  • Check the official Qiskit documentation: qiskit.org/documentation/install
