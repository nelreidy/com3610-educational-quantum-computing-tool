Classical Logic Gates and Circuits

It is helpful to understand classical logic gates and circuits before moving on to quantum gates. Classical logic gates are the building blocks of classical circuits, and are used to perform logical operations on binary numbers. In this section, we will discuss the basic logic gates and their truth tables.

One-Bit Logic Gates

The simplest logic gates in classical computing take one bit as an input, and output one bit. We can also represent logic gates as tables, which you will see in this seciton.

There are two single-bit gates, the identity gate and the NOT gate. The identity gate does nothing to the bit, the state that goes in (A), is the state that comes out (A).

The NOT gate flips the bit - 0 becomes 1, and 1 becomes 0.

There are two other possible one-bit logic gates - the always 1 gate, and the always 0 gate. They are uncommon and do not have a standard diagram.

Two-Bit gates

Two-bit gates, as the name suggests, take two bits as input, and typically have just one output.

The simplest two-bit gate is the AND gate. The AND gate outputs 1 only if both inputs are 1, otherwise it outputs 0.

The OR gate outputs 1 if either input is 1, or if both inputs are 1.

The XOR gate outputs 1 if the inputs are different, and 0 if they are the same.

The NAND gate outputs 0 if both inputs are 1, otherwise it outputs 1.

The NOR gate outputs 0 if either input is 1, or if both inputs are 1.

There are many other two-bit gates, but these are the most common.

Single Qubit Gates

Single qubit gates are the building blocks of quantum circuits. They are used to manipulate the state of a single qubit. In this section, we will discuss the most common single qubit gates and their applications.

The Identity Gate

The identity gate is the simplest quantum gate. Similar to the classical identity gate, it does nothing to the qubit state.

$$ I|0\rangle = |0\rangle $$ $$ I|1\rangle = |1\rangle $$

The Pauli Gates

The Pauli gates are a set of three single qubit gates that are named after the physicist Wolfgang Pauli. They are the Pauli-X gate, the Pauli-Y gate, and the Pauli-Z gate.

The Pauli-X gate is equivalent to the classical NOT gate. On a Bloch sphere, it is a rotation of the qubit state around the x-axis by 180 degrees. So \(|0\rangle\) becomes \(|1\rangle\), and \(|1\rangle\) becomes \(|0\rangle\)
The Pauli-Y gate is a rotation of the qubit state around the y-axis by 180 degrees. It is equivalent to the Pauli-X gate followed by the Pauli-Z gate.
The Pauli-Z gate is a rotation of the qubit state around the z-axis by 180 degrees. It leaves the \(|0\rangle\) state unchanged, and changes the sign of the \(|1\rangle\) state.

$$ Z|0\rangle = |0\rangle $$ $$ Z|1\rangle = -|1\rangle $$

The S Gate and T Gate

The S gate and T gate are two more single qubit gates that are used in quantum circuits. They are both phase gates, and are used to manipulate the phase of a qubit state.

The S gate is equivalent to the square root of the Pauli-Z gate. It is a rotation of the qubit state around the z-axis by 90 degrees.
The T gate is equivalent to the fourth root of the Pauli-Z gate. It is a rotation of the qubit state around the z-axis by 45 degrees.
$$ S|0\rangle = |0\rangle $$ $$ S|1\rangle = i|1\rangle $$

The Hadamard Gate

The Hadamard gate is a single qubit gate that is used to create superposition. It is a rotation of the qubit state around the x-axis by 90 degrees, followed by a rotation around the z-axis by 180 degrees. It is a combination of the Pauli-X and Pauli-Z gates.

$$ H|0\rangle = |+\rangle $$ $$ H|1\rangle = |-\rangle $$

Two Qubit Gates

Two qubit gates are used to manipulate the state of two qubits. Similar to single qubit gates, there are some standard two qubit gates used in quantum computing. In this section, we will discuss the most common two qubit gates and their applications.

The SWAP Gate

The SWAP gate is a two qubit gate that swaps the states of two qubits. It is used to exchange the states of two qubits, and is used in many quantum algorithms.

$$ SWAP|00\rangle = |00\rangle $$ $$ SWAP|01\rangle = |10\rangle $$ $$ SWAP|10\rangle = |01\rangle $$ $$ SWAP|11\rangle = |11\rangle $$

Controlled Gates

Controlled gates are a type of two qubit gate that are used to apply a gate to one qubit, depending on the state of another qubit. The most common controlled gates are the controlled-NOT gate (CNOT gate) and the controlled-Z gate (CZ gate).

CZ Gate
The CNOT gate is a controlled gate that applies the Pauli-X gate to the target qubit if the control qubit is in the \(|1\rangle\) state.
The CZ gate is a controlled gate that applies the Pauli-Z gate to the target qubit if the control qubit is in the \(|1\rangle\) state.
$$ CNOT|00\rangle = |00\rangle $$ $$ CNOT|01\rangle = |01\rangle $$ $$ CNOT|10\rangle = |11\rangle $$ $$ CNOT|11\rangle = |10\rangle $$
$$ CZ|00\rangle = |00\rangle $$ $$ CZ|01\rangle = |01\rangle $$ $$ CZ|10\rangle = |10\rangle $$ $$ CZ|11\rangle = -|11\rangle $$

The CNOT gate is a controlled gate that applies the Pauli-X gate to the target qubit if the control qubit is in the \(|1\rangle\) state.

$$ CNOT|00\rangle = |00\rangle $$ $$ CNOT|01\rangle = |01\rangle $$ $$ CNOT|10\rangle = |11\rangle $$ $$ CNOT|11\rangle = |10\rangle $$

CZ Gate

The CZ gate is a controlled gate that applies the Pauli-Z gate to the target qubit if the control qubit is in the \(|1\rangle\) state.

$$ CZ|00\rangle = |00\rangle $$ $$ CZ|01\rangle = |01\rangle $$ $$ CZ|10\rangle = |10\rangle $$ $$ CZ|11\rangle = -|11\rangle $$

This concept can be applied to any single qubit gate, where a control qubit conditions the target gate to trigger when in the \(|1\rangle\) state. Conversely, an anti-control can be used to condition a gate to trigger when the control is in the \(|0\rangle\) state.
