Introduction

This lesson introduces the fundamental principles and phenomena that govern quantum mechanics and quantum computing. These principles form the backbone of quantum circuits and algorithms, enabling the unique capabilities of quantum computers.

What Should I Know Before Starting?

To get the most out of this lesson, it is helpful to have a basic understanding of quantum mechanics, including:

  • Superposition – The ability of quantum systems to exist in multiple states at once.
  • Entanglement – A deep connection between particles, allowing them to influence each other instantly over any distance.
  • Quantum Measurement – The principle that observing a quantum system affects its state.

If these concepts are new to you, consider starting with our Introduction to Quantum Computing lesson.

The No-Cloning Theorem

The No-Cloning Theorem states that it is impossible to create an identical copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state. Unlike classical information, which can be copied without altering the original (e.g., duplicating a file), quantum information behaves differently due to the fundamental laws of quantum mechanics.

Why Can't We Clone Quantum States?

In classical computing, copying information is straightforward, but in quantum mechanics, the evolution of a quantum system is governed by unitary and linear operations. Suppose we have a qubit:

$$ | \psi \rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle $$

This qubit |ψ⟩ has a probabaility α of being 0 and β of being 1. If we attempt to clone this state, the operation would require:

$$ | \psi \rangle |0\rangle \rightarrow U | \psi \rangle | \psi \rangle $$

where U represents a unitary operator performing the cloning by taking the original qubit |ψ⟩ and an empty qubit |0⟩ and turns them into two identical copies. However, due to the linearity of U, such an operation would lead to inconsistencies in the resulting quantum state. Furthermore, unitary operations preserve probabilities, making it impossible to construct a universal cloning operator that works for all quantum states.

The Role of Measurement

Quantum superposition makes it impossible to determine a qubit’s exact state without measuring it. However, measurement collapses the state, irreversibly destroying the superposition. Since copying requires “knowing” the exact state, it contradicts the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics.

Applications of the No-Cloning Theorem

  • Quantum Cryptography – Any attempt to copy a quantum state in cryptographic protocols would alter it, introducing immediately detectable errors.
  • Quantum Computing Algorithm Integrity – The theorem prevents unintended duplication of quantum states during computation, preserving coherence and reliability.

Quantum Teleportation

Quantum teleportation is a protocol that enables the transfer of a quantum state from one particle to another, regardless of distance, without physically moving the particle itself. It is similar to the teleportation seen in science fiction, but with a quantum twist. Unlike classical communication, which transmits a copy of information directly, quantum teleportation relies on entanglement and classical communication to transfer the complete information about a quantum state from one location to another.

Due to the no-cloning theorem , the original state cannot be copied and must bedestroyed during the process. This ensures that the quantum state is transferred but not duplicated. The physical particle itself does not move—only its quantum state is reconstructed at the destination.

Steps of Quantum Teleportation

  1. Entanglement Preparation: Alice (the sender) and Bob (the receiver) share an entangled Bell state, for example, the maxiamlly enatgled state \( |\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{|00\rangle + |11\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} \) between two qubits (q1 and q2). Alice holds q1, and Bob holds q2.
  2. Measurement and Communication: Alice interacts her part of the entangled pair with the unknown state \( |\psi\rangle \) which we want to teleport, and then then measures both qubits. This is where the original qubit is destroyed, as measurement collapses the quantum state irreversibly. She sends the two-bit measurement result to Bob over a classical communication channel.
  3. State Reconstruction: Based on Alice’s measurement results, Bob applies corrective quantum gates (X for bit-flip, Z for phase-flip) to his qubit, recovering the original quantum state \( |\psi\rangle \).

Applications of Quantum Teleportation

  • Quantum Networks: Quantum teleportation enables the secure transmission of quantum information over long distances, forming the backbone of future quantum internet technologies.
  • General Quantum Computing: It allows qubits to be moved efficiently within a quantum processor or even across multiple quantum devices, ensuring the scalability of quantum computing.

Quantum Tunneling

Quantum tunneling is a phenomenon in which a particle can pass through a potential barrier even when classical physics predicts it does not have enough energy to do so. This occurs because of the wave-like nature of quantum particles, which allows them to exist in a superposition of states, giving them a nonzero probability of appearing on the other side of a barrier.

Understanding the Phenomenon

In classical mechanics, if a ball does not have enough energy to roll over a hill, it will simply stop. However, in quantum mechanics, a particle described by a wavefunction has a probability of "tunneling" through a barrier, appearing on the other side as if it had passed straight through. This is due to the probabilistic nature of wavefunctions and the Schrödinger equation.

Mathematical Representation

Quantum tunneling is described using the time-independent Schrödinger equation. If a particle with energy \( E \) approaches a potential barrier of height \( V \), where \( E < V \), its wavefunction in the barrier region is given by:

$$ \psi(x) = Ae^{-\kappa x} $$ where \( \kappa = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V - E)}}{\hbar} \)
\( A \) is the normalization constant.
\( \hbar \) is the reduced Planck constant.
\( m \) is the mass of the particle.

The wavefunction decays exponentially within the barrier, but does not reach zero, meaning there is a probability that the particle will be found on the other side.

Applications of Quantum Tunneling

  • Transistors and Microelectronics: Quantum tunneling is fundamental to modern semiconductor devices, such as field-effect transistors and tunnel diodes, where electrons tunnel through insulating barriers.
  • Nuclear Fusion: Quantum tunneling allows protons in the Sun to overcome their electrostatic repulsion, enabling nuclear fusion and energy generation.
  • Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM): STM devices use quantum tunneling to visualize surfaces at the atomic level, by measuring the tunneling current between a sharp tip and a conductive surface.

Bell's Theorem and Non-Locality

Bell’s Theorem challenges our classical understanding of reality by showing that quantum mechanics predicts correlations between entangled particles that cannot be explained by classical physics. It tests the concept of local realism, which consists of:

  • Locality: An event happening at one location cannot instantly affect an event at another distant location (i.e., information cannot travel faster than light).
  • Realism: Particles have definite properties (like spin or polarization) before they are measured, independent of observation.

Bell’s Inequality

Bell’s inequality sets a mathematical limit on how strongly two distant particles can be correlated under the assumption of local realism. If local realism were true, measurements on entangled particles should not exceed this limit. However, experiments have demonstrated that entangled particles violate Bell’s inequality, meaning their correlations are stronger than what local realism allows. This suggests that at least one of the following must be false:

  • Locality is wrong: There is some kind of instantaneous connection between entangled particles.
  • Realism is wrong: Quantum properties are not predetermined but only defined when measured.

Implications of Bell’s Theorem

Bell’s Theorem confirms that quantum mechanics is fundamentally non-local. The violation of Bell’s inequality implies that entangled particles behave in a way that cannot be explained by any classical local theory, supporting the idea that quantum entanglement allows for correlations that defy classical intuitions about space and time. I.e measurements on one particle can instantaneously affect another, regardless of distance.

Applications of Bell’s Theorem

  • Quantum Cryptography: Bell’s theorem underpins quantum key distribution protocols, such as BB84 and device-independent cryptography, ensuring secure communication based on quantum entanglement.
  • Quantum Networks: Future quantum networks use entanglement to establish non-local correlations between distant nodes, enabling ultra-secure global communication.
